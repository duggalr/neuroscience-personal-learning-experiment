## 1. The lens: thinking in levels
*How to reason about the brain before learning any biology.*

- **Levels of analysis**: molecular → cellular → circuit → systems → behavioral/clinical; each level answers a different kind of question.
  - **Complementary explanations**: explanations at different levels stack and complement, they don't compete.
  - **Cross-level inference**: a result at one level does not automatically explain another; the bridge needs evidence.
  - **Constrained links**: cross-level connections are limited by plausible biological paths (this is what makes neuroscience *structured*).
  - **Bridge strength**: grade how strongly a paper's evidence actually supports its cross-level claim.
  - **Developmental time**: a dimension that cuts *across* levels, not a separate level.
- **Measurement vs claim**: keep "what was manipulated", "what was measured", and "what is claimed" distinct.
- **Many-to-one / one-to-many**: one gene ≠ one disorder; mappings run both ways.

## 2. The causal ladder: genes → phenotype
*The chain that connects molecules to observable traits.*

- **Gene**: a DNA sequence of instructions (the gene is *not* the protein or the trait).
  - **Ion channel gene/protein**: a gene encodes an ion channel protein; the gene is not the channel.
  - **De novo variant**: a DNA change new in a child, not inherited from either parent.
- **Genes → proteins → neurons → circuits → systems**: each level shapes the next, with feedback (not a one-way pipeline).
- **Phenotype**: a measurable trait or outcome (behavior, cognition, symptoms, diagnosis).

## 3. The neuron: the building block
*The unit of computation, its parts and what they do.*

- **Neuron polarity**: a neuron is a polarized cell whose parts are specialized for different jobs.
- **Parts**
  - **Dendrites**: the branched input region; a large surface for many synaptic inputs.
  - **Soma**: the cell body, holding the nucleus and metabolic machinery.
  - **Axon**: the output cable that carries signals away from the soma.
  - **Axon initial segment (AIS)**: the zone just past the soma where spikes usually begin.
  - **Synapse**: the junction where one neuron influences another cell.
- **Function**
  - **Input → output transformation**: a neuron converts many inputs into one selective output (the basic unit of neural computation).
  - **Excitation vs inhibition**: excitatory and inhibitory inputs shape output differently.
  - **Dendritic integration**: *where* a synapse sits on the dendritic tree changes how much it matters.
  - **Backpropagating action potential**: a spike from the AIS travels forward down the axon and backward into the dendrites.

## 4. Circuits and systems
*Neurons assembled into networks and large-scale function.*

- **Circuit**: an organized network with specific partners, strengths, timings, and cell types (not all-to-all).
- **Systems level**: how multiple brain regions coordinate to support a function.

## 5. Disorders
*Where the foundations point: intellectual disability, epilepsy, autism.*

- **Neurodevelopmental disorder**: a disruption of brain development and circuit formation.
- **Clinical disorder**: defined by a *pattern* of symptoms/development, not a single molecule.

## 6. Modeling neurons
*Turning biology into something you can simulate.*

- **Computational neuron model**: judged by which features it preserves for a question; all models trade realism vs interpretability vs cost.
  - **Model class fits purpose**: Hodgkin-Huxley (mechanistic excitability), compartmental/NEURON (morphology), LIF (efficiency), Brian2 (fast prototyping), NEST (large networks).

## The skill that runs through everything
- **Paper-reading workflow**: tag each result by its level, identify the claimed bridge, grade the bridge's strength. A repeatable three-step loop for reading real papers.
